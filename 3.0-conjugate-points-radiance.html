Conjugate Points and Radiance

Two points are conjugate when they are symmetrically opposite each other, with respect to the centre of the box.

Consider an object that travels back and forth between a point and its conjugate.
  • per completed cycle, the object has energy proportional to the square of the euclidean distance travelled
  • the square of the euclidean distance from a point to its conjugate is the radiance of the point
  • the sum of the radiance of every point in the box is the box radiance
  • the radiance is a maximal value


The radiance can be represented in a euclidean diagram, or in a radiance diagram where all the points are stretched out in a single line in index order.

The euclidean radiance is a function of the bases of the box whereas the index radiance is a function of the volume only.

The radiance can be considered as an operator that swaps each point with its conjugate.

Applying the radiance operator twice returns each point to its starting place - equivalent to the identity operator.
