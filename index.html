Thinking inside the Box

Tally Ho!

To tally a box means to systematically count the items (or points) inside.

The process seems obvious:
  1. assign an initial tally value of 0
  2. unpack each point
    1. assign the current tally value to the current point
    2. increase the tally by 1
  3. collect the final tally
  4. assert that the final tally is equal to the volume of the box
At the end of the process, each point will have been assigned a unique tally value, from 0 up to one less than the volume of the box - an index value.

This article will consider:
  • the different ways to tally/index a box - place value tables
  • the relationships between different place value tables - resonances
  • the group actions that arise
  • a physical interpretation as energy quanta and spin

Place Value Tables

A place value table is familiar in base 10 as representing the sum of completed powers of 10; a fragment of the table for the box 10 x 10 x 10 is shown below:

Every point occurs as a row in the table and each row provides a coordinate for the point.

Conjugate Points and Radiance

Two points are conjugate when they are symmetrically opposite each other, with respect to the centre of the box.

  • the radiance of a point is the square of the euclidean distance from the point to it's conjugate point.
  • the sum of the radiance of every point in a box gives the total radiance of the box
  • the total radiance of the box represents a maximum energy that can be contained in the box

Two Dimensional Boxes

There are two ways to unpack the two-dimensional box 5 x 3; either counting 5 lots of 3, or 3 lots of 5. Either way must tally to the same volume, but each way may assign different tally values to some points. This is seen by aligning rows from corresponding place values tables (NB: the second place table is reversed):

In this case, both orbits are self-conjugate, the member points accompany, and are opposite their conjugates. Self-conjugate orbits are indicated (in the table) by splitting the orbit member sequence into two equal parts and laying the parts over each other so that each point lies over its conjugate. Therefore, a self-conjugate orbit cannot have an odd number of members.

The fixed points and orbits can be visualized in a 2 dimensional grid, and in a radiance diagram:
  • The euclidean perimeter of each orbit is the distance travelled for a complete revolution - hence a velocity
  • The square of the perimeter is therefore proportional to some quanta of energy, the inverse orbit having a corresponding negative relative value
  • The sum of the absolute value of the index jumps of each orbit is the index distance (perimeter) travelled for a complete revolution
